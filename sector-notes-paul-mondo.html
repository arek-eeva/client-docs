Sector Notes

A Conversation with Paul Mondo

Five observations on the state of early childhood education in Australia

Paul Mondo
National President, Australian Childcare Alliance
Managing Director, Bimbi Early Learning and Deer Park Early Learning

Notes captured by: Arek Rainczuk, EEVA
Date: 21 May 2026

Paul Mondo has been on the floor of the early childhood sector for more than twenty years. He runs two centres in Melbourne, sits on the ACA Victoria board, and currently serves as National President of the Australian Childcare Alliance. There are few people in Australia with a clearer view of where the sector is now, and where it is going.

In a recent conversation, he shared his read on the current climate, the structural changes underway, and what they mean for operators who are paying attention. What follows is a short summary of five things he said that we thought were worth keeping.

ONE

The sector is in a "really financially vulnerable position."

"It's not equal everywhere, but the sector's in a heightened level of stress and anxiety."

Paul's read on the national picture is direct. Oversupply is real in most parts of metropolitan Australia, despite what the media says. Public pressure around child safety has caused some families to delay entry into the system. Regulatory implementation has added cost. Services that have built occupancy on word of mouth alone are finding that the old model does not return calls.

The result is a vicious circle. Services clearly need to invest in marketing to grow occupancy, but they may not have the discretionary funds to do that. Strong local reputations still matter, but they can flip quickly when something goes wrong. And, as Paul put it, "you employ humans, and humans do the wrong thing sometimes."

TWO

Small family-owned providers have a distinct advantage right now.

"We don't have to say we're not them. We say this is who we are."

This was perhaps the sharpest strategic observation Paul made. The reputational damage to the big chains, particularly through media coverage, has shifted the market. Small operators no longer have to defend themselves against the national narrative. They can simply tell their own story, and the contrast does the work.

The advantage is not theoretical. Large chains may have advanced marketing teams "with a lot of money to throw at it," but, in Paul's words, "when you rip the layer off, that's not the lived experience." For a small family-owned centre, the lived experience is the marketing. The question, he said, is whether operators know how to articulate their point of difference. "That's the underlying trigger for all of your decisions."

THREE

The conversion cycle has three parts: Engagement, Lead-In, Evolution.

"You can be great there, but if you don't bring people in, it doesn't matter how good you are."

Paul described the family enrolment journey as a cycle, and the language is worth borrowing. Engagement is everything that gets a family to consider you. Lead-In is the moment they make contact and you bring them through the door. Evolution is what happens from the first interaction onward, through tour, decision, and enrolment.

His conviction is that each link is essential, and that operators tend to underinvest in whichever one they are weakest at. At his own centres, post-tour conversion is approaching ninety-five percent, which tells him the front-end work has earned the right kind of attention. "The first interaction is as important as getting them in, because you can turn people away pretty quickly in that first moment."

FOUR

The first phone call is where centres quietly lose families.

"I've had new managers ring random centres and pretend to be a parent."

This was Paul's favourite tactical insight, and one of the most useful for any operator to absorb. He runs mystery shopping calls on his own managers as part of their development. The pattern, he said, is depressingly consistent.

"The amount of times you get someone pick up, 'oh, they're not available, can you call back later,' being looped around and not going anywhere." For a family already on the fence, that first call can be the entire decision. The opposite is also true. A professional, warm, decisive first interaction can carry a centre through a tour that might otherwise have been merely fine.

The fix is not complicated, but it does have to be deliberate: the first phone call is a piece of the service, not an interruption to it.

FIVE

Marketing content without children is becoming the right approach.

"I would be very comfortable as a sector if we actually banned any online presence of any children."

This is where Paul's read of the sector becomes forward-looking, and where many operators have not yet caught up. His own marketing approach is now strict: no children in any video or photo content. Shoots happen on a Saturday. The content focuses on family ownership, staff, centre managers, daily rhythms of the environment, and the centre's history.

The motivation is partly compliance and partly something deeper. "From a marketing perspective, it works so much better where you can show the day in action. But it's just not the path of our future as a sector." Paul thinks the sector should move further than the regulator currently asks. Many operators are still using children-in-content as their default, and the gap between that approach and where Paul thinks the sector is heading is going to widen.

For centres ready to commit to a no-children content method, the creative work does not become weaker. It becomes more focused on the people and the values that actually do the work of running a centre.

Thank you, Paul.
